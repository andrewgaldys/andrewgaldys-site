Investing 101

By Andrew Galdys

01 / Philosophy of Simplicity

When you spend your days studying economics and working in real estate, you quickly realize that wealth isn't built through complex, secret strategies. The finance industry makes billions of dollars every year by convincing you that investing is too complicated to do on your own. It isn't.

As Morgan Housel writes in The Psychology of Money: "Doing well with money has a little to do with how smart you are and a lot to do with how you behave."

The core rule is simple: a high savings rate, routed ruthlessly into low-fee index funds, left alone for decades. You do not need to time the market, pick the next hot stock, or read daily financial news. In fact, doing those things actively destroys your returns.

"Don't look for the needle in the haystack. Just buy the haystack."
— John C. Bogle, Founder of Vanguard

02 / Data Against Active Management

Wall Street hires the smartest mathematicians in the world to pick stocks, and they still fail. According to the S&P Indices Versus Active (SPIVA) scorecard, over a 15-year period, roughly 90% of actively managed mutual funds underperform the basic S&P 500 index.

If the professionals, who have supercomputers and insider access, cannot beat the market average, neither can you or I. The smartest move is to simply own the entire market and let American business do the heavy lifting for you.

03 / Infrastructure

Your money needs to live in the right accounts to protect it from inflation and unnecessary taxes. I personally use and recommend Fidelity for all of this because their platform is clean, their customer service is excellent, and their index funds have zero minimums.

  • 1. The High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA) This is the holding pen for your emergency cash. Traditional banks pay you 0.01% interest, meaning you are actively losing purchasing power to inflation. Moving your cash to a HYSA (earning 4% to 5%) is a mandatory first step.
  • 2. The Roth IRA (The Tax Shelter) A Roth IRA is not an investment; it is a bucket you put investments into. You put post-tax money in, it grows completely tax-free, and you withdraw it tax-free at retirement.

    The Math: If you invest just $500 a month starting at age 19, assuming an 8% average return, you will have roughly $2.8 million by age 65. Because it is in a Roth IRA, over $2.5 million of that growth is entirely legally tax-free.
  • 3. The Standard Brokerage Account This is for capital you want to invest, but might need access to before retirement—whether that is for buying investment real estate in five years or funding a business venture.

04 / Cost of Fees

When buying index funds, the most important metric is the Expense Ratio (the fee the fund charges you). Fees look small on paper, but they compound just like interest does.

Let’s look at a $100,000 portfolio growing at 8% over 30 years:

  • With a 0.03% fee (a standard Fidelity index fund), your final balance is roughly $993,000.
  • With a 1.00% fee (a standard financial advisor fee), your final balance is roughly $761,000.

That 1% fee just stole $232,000 from your future net worth. Keep your fees near zero.
